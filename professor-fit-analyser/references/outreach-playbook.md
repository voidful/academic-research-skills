# Outreach Playbook

## Core principle

The email should show concrete fit, not admiration alone.

## Minimum ingredients

Every strong email should include:
1. a precise subject line,
2. a brief self-introduction,
3. one or two concrete professor research lines,
4. one concrete way the sender can contribute,
5. a modest call to action.

## Subject line patterns

### PhD applicant
- prospective phd applicant interested in [research line]
- applicant for [year or term], fit in [research line]

### Master's thesis student
- prospective master's thesis student — interest in [research line]
- master's student seeking thesis advisor in [research line]

### Undergraduate researcher (capstone/thesis/專題)
- undergraduate research opportunity — interest in [research line]
- [department] undergraduate seeking research experience in [topic]

### Research collaborator
- possible collaboration on [problem or method]
- exploring overlap in [topic] and [topic]

### Short networking email (any level)
- quick question on [specific paper or direction]
- brief note on overlap with your work on [topic]

## Opening paragraph rules

Good opening:
- states who the sender is,
- states the purpose in one sentence,
- anchors the email in a specific research line.

Bad opening:
- long autobiography,
- generic praise,
- multiple paragraphs before any concrete fit.

## Research reference rules

Mention one or two research lines, not five papers.
Summarize the line in your own words. Do not paste mini paper summaries.
The goal is to prove understanding, not to impress with volume.

## Contribution rules

The sender must offer one of the following. Select the types that are appropriate for the field and the sender's level.

Universal contribution types:
- a **methodological skill**: expertise in a technique, tool, or analytical approach that the professor's group uses or needs.
- a **theoretical angle**: formal or conceptual depth that could strengthen the group's foundations.
- a **domain bridge**: expertise in a different field or application area that could open new directions.
- a **resource or data contribution**: access to data, corpora, equipment, field sites, clinical populations, or other hard-to-obtain resources.
- a **language or cultural expertise**: fluency in languages relevant to the research (for archival, comparative, or international studies), or deep familiarity with a specific culture, region, or community.
- a **clinical or applied experience**: relevant clinical training, patient interaction, or professional practice experience (for biomedical, nursing, public health, or applied fields).
- a **learning eagerness** (for undergraduates and early master's students): strong foundational skills, relevant coursework, and willingness to invest time in learning. This is not as compelling as concrete expertise, but honesty about your level combined with specific skills is valued.

Field-specific examples:
- CS/ML: engineering strength (systems building, large-scale training), benchmark or dataset construction, model design expertise.
- Biology/medicine: wet-lab skills (PCR, cell culture, animal models, microscopy), clinical access, specific organism or model system expertise, bioinformatics pipelines, biostatistics.
- Clinical/translational medicine: IRB/ethics committee experience, patient cohort access, clinical trial design, systematic review methodology, evidence-based practice.
- Public health/epidemiology: epidemiological methods, survey design, access to health data registries, GIS for health mapping, qualitative health research.
- Psychology: experimental design, psychometrics, fMRI/EEG experience, clinical assessment tools, statistical methods (SEM, HLM, Bayesian).
- Social sciences: survey design, qualitative methods (ethnography, interviews, focus groups), access to populations, econometric techniques, policy connections, archival research.
- Humanities: archival or language expertise, paleographic skills, theoretical traditions (critical theory, postcolonialism, feminism, etc.), digital humanities tools, close-reading and textual analysis ability, translation skills.
- History: archival research experience, reading knowledge of relevant languages, familiarity with specific historical periods or geographic regions, oral history methodology.
- Law: legal research methodology, case law analysis, comparative law expertise, policy analysis.
- Arts: studio practice, curation experience, performance skills, critical writing about art/music/film.
- Physics/math: proof techniques, simulation expertise, experimental apparatus skills.

### Level-specific contribution framing

**PhD applicants**: Emphasize concrete research contributions — methods you can bring, problems you understand deeply, resources you can access.

**Master's students**: Emphasize relevant coursework, project experience, and specific technical skills. Show you understand the research area well enough to tackle a thesis-scale problem.

**Undergraduate students**: Emphasize foundational skills (programming languages, lab techniques, analytical tools, relevant coursework), enthusiasm, and reliability. It is perfectly acceptable to say "I am still learning, but I have X background and I am eager to contribute to Y." Professors taking undergraduates expect to invest in teaching; what they evaluate is whether you have enough foundation to benefit and whether you will be committed.

The contribution should connect to the professor's likely future direction, not only old papers.

## Tone rules

Use:
- specific,
- respectful,
- calm,
- direct.

Avoid:
- desperation,
- exaggerated humility,
- sales language,
- entitlement,
- empty claims about passion.

## Interview preparation rules

Prepare three stories (calibrate to your level):

**PhD applicants**:
1. a research project you drove,
2. a project that failed and what you learned,
3. a research idea related to the professor that is small but real.

**Master's students**:
1. a course project or thesis-related work you are proud of,
2. a challenging problem you worked through (technical or intellectual),
3. a specific topic in the professor's research area that excites you and why.

**Undergraduate students**:
1. a course project, personal project, or competition you are proud of,
2. a time you learned something difficult on your own,
3. a specific thing about the professor's research that you want to learn more about.

## Smart questions to ask the professor

Ask about:
- how the group chooses problems,
- what makes a student successful there,
- where the group is expanding next,
- what open bottlenecks remain in the research line.

For undergraduate students, add:
- whether there are currently undergraduates in the group,
- what a typical undergraduate project looks like,
- what preparation would be most useful before starting.

Avoid asking only about rankings, money, or prestige in the first conversation unless the context clearly requires it.

## Counter-questioning strategy

Questions to probe mentoring style:
- "What does a typical first-year project look like in your group?"
- "How often do you usually meet with students one-on-one?"
- "How do students in your group typically choose their research topics?"
- "What does success look like for a student in your lab after two years?"

Questions to probe lab/group culture:
- "What does a typical week look like for your students?"
- "How does your group handle major publication deadlines or project milestones?"
- "Do students collaborate with each other on projects, or mostly work independently?"
- "Is there a reading group, journal club, or regular seminar?"

Questions to probe resource and opportunity access (adapt to field):
- "What resources does the group typically use for research?" (compute, equipment, data, field access, library access, etc.)
- "Do students get opportunities to collaborate with other groups, industry, or institutions?"
- "Are there teaching, mentoring, or clinical responsibilities?"
- "How is authorship typically determined in your group?"

## Building rapport in 20 minutes

Structure a short meeting for maximum impact:

1. **(2 min) Specific observation** — Open with a genuine insight about their recent work. Not "I loved your paper" but "I noticed your recent work on X shifts from Y to Z, and I am curious whether that was driven by..."
2. **(5 min) Your relevant background** — One project you drove, one skill, one connection point to their research line.
3. **(5 min) A small research angle** — Float an idea that connects to their work but leverages your unique background. Frame it as thinking-out-loud, not a proposal.
4. **(5 min) Their perspective** — "Where do you see this direction going?" / "What is the main bottleneck right now?" Let them talk. Listen for signals about what they need.
5. **(3 min) Practical close** — "Would it make sense for me to read X before we talk again?" / "Is there a project where you think my background could be useful?"
