# Evidence Rubric

## Evidence ladder

Use this ladder for every non-trivial claim.

### Level A. direct evidence
The claim is directly supported by the source.
Examples:
- the paper states the dataset size,
- the lab website lists the student as a PhD student,
- the official proceedings or journal confirm the venue,
- the grant database shows the funding amount,
- the professor's CV lists a book published by a university press,
- ClinicalTrials.gov lists the professor as PI on an active trial,
- the department page shows the professor as dissertation committee chair for specific students.

### Level B. strong indirect evidence
The claim is not stated once, but repeated signals strongly support it.
Examples:
- the same person appears as first author across several years and is listed on the lab site,
- the same external collaborator appears across a long research line,
- multiple papers acknowledge the same funding source,
- a student's name appears in conference acknowledgments repeatedly before appearing as coauthor (likely progression from RA to researcher),
- the professor's book reviews consistently cite the same theoretical framework, suggesting strong school-of-thought alignment,
- multiple publications from the same clinical cohort over several years suggest ongoing access to a specific patient population.

### Level C. reasonable inference
The claim is plausible but not fully confirmed.
Examples:
- likely resource intensity inferred from project scale and experiment scope,
- likely advising pattern inferred from repeated student authorship growth,
- likely lab culture inferred from publication rhythm and acknowledgment patterns,
- likely theoretical orientation inferred from consistent citation patterns and methodology choices,
- likely clinical access inferred from the types of patient populations studied across multiple papers,
- likely writing mentorship quality inferred from progressive improvement in student-led publications.

### Level D. speculation
The claim is weakly supported and should be labeled as speculative.
Examples:
- lab culture inferred from a single publication burst,
- personality traits inferred from writing style alone,
- mentoring quality inferred from a single student's trajectory,
- theoretical flexibility inferred from a single interdisciplinary paper,
- clinical availability inferred from the professor's listed hospital affiliation alone,
- advising quality for undergraduates inferred from PhD-level mentoring patterns.

## Confidence scale

### High
Use only when the judgment relies mostly on Level A or repeated Level B evidence.

### Medium
Use when the judgment combines some direct evidence with reasonable inferences, or when the evidence is consistent but incomplete.

### Low
Use when the judgment depends on sparse, noisy, or conflicting evidence.

## Counter-evidence protocol

For any major judgment, provide five items:

- **claim**: the judgment being made.
- **supporting evidence**: cite specific papers, sources, or patterns.
- **alternative explanations** (minimum 2-3): present at least two plausible alternative interpretations for ambiguous signals. For example:
  - High publication rate could mean: (1) productive and well-resourced group, (2) fragmented output culture, (3) large group with many members contributing independently.
  - Few student lead-author papers could mean: (1) professor dominates authorship, (2) students are early-stage and not yet ready, (3) field convention differs (alphabetical order, large-team norms, or solo-author tradition).
  - Frequent collaboration with industry could mean: (1) strong applied impact, (2) funding dependency, (3) students get real-world exposure.
  - Long gaps between publications could mean: (1) a book or monograph in preparation, (2) a major grant transition, (3) loss of momentum or personal circumstances, (4) extensive fieldwork or clinical data collection.
  - Solo authorship on most papers could mean: (1) field norm (humanities, math), (2) difficulty collaborating, (3) professor does not involve students in publication.
  - Many case reports in a biomedical professor's record could mean: (1) clinical observation is their research mode, (2) they contribute to clinical knowledge in a different way than bench researchers, (3) they lack access to hypothesis-driven research resources.
  - No visible students in a humanities professor's work could mean: (1) field norm of solo scholarship, (2) students contribute through reading groups and seminars without coauthorship, (3) professor does not invest in student development.
  - Publications in lower-tier journals could mean: (1) the professor targets specialized or regional journals that serve their field well, (2) the professor's work is interdisciplinary and placed in niche venues, (3) declining research productivity.
- **weighted judgment**: after presenting alternatives, state which explanation you consider most likely and why, with a rough confidence weight.
- **what would reduce uncertainty**: specific actions or information that could distinguish between the alternatives.

### Ambiguity threshold rule
If supporting evidence and counter-evidence are roughly equally strong, do NOT default to the positive interpretation. State the ambiguity explicitly and recommend the user investigate further (e.g., talk to current students, read lab news, check field-specific community forums).

## High-risk inference areas

Apply extra caution to these topics:
- mentoring quality,
- workload or overwork,
- student contribution share,
- lab funding level,
- ethics or scientific integrity,
- interpersonal style,
- clinical availability and time commitment (for biomedical professors who are also clinicians),
- theoretical openness and intellectual flexibility (for humanities and social science professors),
- departmental politics and hiring prospects for graduates (all fields, but especially humanities where academic job market is tight).

## Field-specific interpretation warnings

- Do not interpret low output volume as low productivity in fields where books, monographs, or single definitive papers are the standard unit of contribution.
- Do not interpret solo authorship as evidence of poor mentoring in fields where solo work is the norm.
- Do not interpret alphabetical author order as evidence of equal contribution in fields where alphabetical ordering is convention.
- Do not interpret absence of preprints as evidence of secrecy in fields where preprint culture is not established.
- Do not interpret the absence of a "lab" as lack of research activity in humanities, social sciences, or theoretical fields where the term "lab" does not apply.
- Do not interpret a mix of clinical and research publications as lack of research focus in clinical medicine — clinician-scientists operate in both domains by design.
- Do not interpret the absence of computational work as a weakness in fields where the research is archival, interpretive, ethnographic, or based on close reading.
- Do not interpret conference presentations (without proceedings) as inferior output in humanities and social sciences where conferences serve networking and feedback functions rather than publication functions.
- Do not interpret the absence of external grants as evidence of low productivity in fields or countries where research is funded through institutional budgets, sabbaticals, or smaller internal grants.

## Mandatory language cues

Use phrases like:
- "directly supported by"
- "suggested by repeated evidence"
- "reasonable inference"
- "cannot be concluded from public evidence alone"
